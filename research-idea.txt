RoadFM-Lite
A Self-Supervised Foundation Model for Road-Network-Grounded
Trajectory Representation with Application to Sybil Attack Detection
Master's Thesis Proposal
1. Problem Statement
Vehicular crowdsensing (VCS) systems compensate drivers for gathering traffic and
environmental data, but they are vulnerable to Sybil attacks in which a single adversary forges
multiple fake vehicle identities to steal rewards and corrupt data quality. Detecting these
attacks requires distinguishing fabricated trajectories from legitimate ones. Existing trajectory
foundation models such as TrajFM and UniTraj treat trajectories as sequences of coordinates
or point-of-interest tokens, largely ignoring the physical road network structure (topology,
speed limits, lane counts, road classification) on which vehicles actually travel.
This thesis proposes RoadFM-Lite, a self-supervised foundation model that jointly encodes
road-network graph structure and trajectory kinematics into a shared representation. The
central hypothesis is that grounding trajectory representations in road-network features
produces embeddings that transfer more effectively to Sybil detection tasks—especially under
limited labeled data (few-shot) and cross-region transfer settings—than trajectory-only
encoders.
2. Research Question
Does a self-supervised model that jointly encodes road-network structure and trajectory
kinematics produce representations that transfer more effectively to vehicular Sybil detection
tasks than trajectory-only models, particularly under few-shot supervision and cross-region
deployment?
3. Proposed Architecture
RoadFM-Lite uses a two-branch architecture fused into a unified trajectory representation:
Branch 1 — Road Network Encoder (Graph)
The road network is extracted from OpenStreetMap and represented as a graph where nodes
correspond to road segments and edges represent connectivity. Node features include speed
limit, lane count, road class (residential, arterial, highway), segment length, and curvature. A
2–3 layer GraphSAGE encoder produces a fixed embedding per road segment. These
embeddings are precomputed once per city and frozen during trajectory training, avoiding the
complexity of joint end-to-end graph training.
Branch 2 — Trajectory Encoder (Sequence)
Each vehicle trajectory is a time series of (position, velocity, acceleration, heading,
timestamp) map-matched to road segments. A Transformer-based encoder (TimesNet or
vanilla Transformer) processes the sequence. At each trajectory time step, the corresponding
road-segment embedding from Branch 1 is concatenated to the kinematic features before
encoding. This grounding ensures the model always knows the physical context of the road
the vehicle is on—for example, distinguishing a 25 mph residential street from a 70 mph
highway.
Fusion and Output
The concatenation of road-segment embeddings with kinematic features at each time step
serves as the fusion mechanism. A projection head is used during pretraining, which is
replaced by a classification or contrastive head during downstream fine-tuning for Sybil
detection.
4. Pretraining Objectives
The model is pretrained on large volumes of unlabeled trajectory data using two
self-supervised objectives:
Masked Trajectory Reconstruction. Randomly mask 15% of time steps and train the
encoder to reconstruct position and velocity at the masked locations. The road-segment
embeddings provide context about what motion is physically plausible on each segment,
forcing the model to learn road-conditioned dynamics rather than memorizing coordinate
patterns.
Physical Constraint Prediction. At each time step, predict whether the trajectory point
violates the road segment's speed limit or exhibits acceleration inconsistent with road
geometry (e.g., high speed on a sharp curve). Labels are generated automatically from
the data by comparing kinematic values against road attributes, requiring no manual
annotation.
5. Downstream Task: Sybil Detection
After pretraining, the encoder is fine-tuned for Sybil trajectory detection using the VeReMi
Extension dataset under two evaluation settings:
Few-Shot Contrastive Fine-Tuning. Use 5, 10, and 20 labeled examples per class with
a supervised contrastive loss (InfoNCE). The goal is to demonstrate that road-grounded
representations require fewer labeled attack samples to learn an effective decision
boundary compared to trajectory-only baselines.
Zero-Shot Retrieval-Based Detection. Maintain a memory bank of verified normal
trajectory embeddings. Flag incoming trajectories whose embedding distance to nearest
normal neighbors exceeds a threshold. Road grounding should reduce false positives by
helping the model distinguish "unusual but physically valid routes" from "physically
impossible trajectories."

The key comparison is RoadFM-Lite vs. the trajectory-only TimesNet encoder. Beating this
baseline validates that road-network grounding provides a measurable advantage for Sybil
detection.
7. Evaluation Plan
Detection Metrics: Precision, recall, F1-score, and AUROC on the VeReMi Extension
dataset across all attack types.
Few-Shot Analysis: Detection performance as a function of labeled examples per class (5,
10, 20) to measure label efficiency.
Cross-City Transfer Experiment: Pretrain on City A (SUMO-simulated or NGSIM data),
fine-tune on City B (different road network and traffic patterns). This is the strongest thesis
contribution—it tests whether road-grounded representations generalize across cities
because the model has learned abstract concepts like "trajectory on highway" vs. "trajectory
on residential street" rather than memorizing specific coordinates.
Ablation Study: Compare performance with and without road-segment embeddings, and with
individual pretraining objectives removed, to quantify the contribution of each component.

10. Expected Contributions
1. Road-grounded trajectory representations. A self-supervised encoder that fuses
road-network graph embeddings with trajectory kinematics, producing representations that
encode both motion dynamics and physical road context.
2. Empirical validation for Sybil detection. Controlled experiments demonstrating that
road-network grounding improves Sybil detection accuracy, label efficiency, and cross-region
transferability compared to trajectory-only models.
3. Pretrained weights and open pipeline. Publicly released pretrained encoder weights,
data-processing pipeline, and evaluation protocols that can serve as the detection backbone
for the broader Sybil Guard federated defense framework.
11. Connection to Sybil Guard Project
This thesis produces the pretrained trajectory encoder required by Thrust 2 of the Sybil Guard
grant proposal, but with a deeper treatment of road-network integration than the proposal's
current TimesNet-only design. The pretrained weights become a project deliverable, the
few-shot evaluation validates the proposal's claim about minimal supervision, and the
cross-city transfer experiments support the proposal's claim about deployment across
heterogeneous traffic environments. The encoder architecture is designed to slot directly into
the personalized federated learning framework described in the proposal.
12. Publication Target
One conference paper targeting IEEE ICC, IEEE VTC, ACM SIGSPATIAL, or a workshop at
NDSS/CCS on vehicular security. The paper presents the road-grounded encoder and
demonstrates that it outperforms trajectory-only models for Sybil detection under few-shot and
transfer settings.
